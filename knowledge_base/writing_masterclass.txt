TITLE: Story: Substance, Structure, Style, and the Principles of Screenwriting
AUTHOR: Robert McKee
SOURCE: PDF Masterclass (The "Screenwriter's Bible")
KEY_INSIGHTS:
- **The Structure Triangle**: 
    - *Archplot*: Causality, closed endings, linear time, external conflict, active protagonist.
    - *Miniplot*: Open endings, internal conflict, passive protagonist, minimalism.
    - *Antiplot*: Coincidence, nonlinear time, inconsistent realities (anti-structure).
- **True Character**: Appearance is NOT reality. True character is revealed through CHOICES under PRESSURE. Characterization (IQ, age, style) is just the surface.
- **The 5-Part Design**: 
    1. *Inciting Incident*: Upsets the balance of life.
    2. *Progressive Complications*: Events passing points of no return.
    3. *Crisis*: The final decision / the choice between irreconcilable goods or the lesser of two evils.
    4. *Climax*: Absolute, irreversible change in values.
    5. *Resolution*: Trailing material following the climax.
- **The Principle of Antagonism**: A story is only as compelling as its forces of antagonism. 
- **Negation of the Negation**: The ultimate "dark" force. (e.g. Justice -> Unfairness -> Injustice -> Tyranny).
- **The Gap**: "Story is born in that place where the subjective and objective realms touch." The gap between a character's expectation of an action and the actual result.
- **Exposition**: "Show, Don't Tell." Never force words into a character's mouth to tell the audience something they don't want to know. Convert exposition to "ammunition" for conflict.

TRANSCRIPT EXCERPT:
TRUE CHARACTER is revealed in the choices a human being makes under pressure—the greater the pressure, the deeper the revelation.
STORYTELLING is the creative demonstration of truth. A story is the living proof of an idea, the conversion of idea to action.
The function of STRUCTURE is to provide progressively building pressures that force characters into more and more difficult dilemmas.
The measure of the value of a character’s desire is in direct proportion to the risk he’s willing to take to achieve it.

TITLE: The Soul of Screenwriting: On Writing, Dramatic Truth, and Knowing Yourself
AUTHOR: Keith Cunningham
SOURCE: PDF Masterclass (Deep Character & Thematic Focus)
KEY_INSIGHTS:
- **The Soul**: The screenplay's "soul" is not plot but the protagonist's INNER JOURNEY—what they learn about themselves.
- **The Character Spine**: Every great story has a character with a SPINE: a deep-seated psychological flaw or wound driving their behavior.
- **The 3-Phase Transformation**:
    1. *Denial*: The character lives in a false identity, driven by the flaw/wound.
    2. *Crisis*: Events force confrontation with the truth about themselves.
    3. *Integration*: The character accepts a new truth and transforms (or refuses and tragically fails).
- **Dramatic Truth**: The screenplay must hold a "dramatic truth"—a universal human insight that resonates beyond the specific plot.
- **The Controlling Idea**: A single sentence encapsulating the story's meaning, structured as: "[Value] is achieved/lost when [Cause]." (e.g., "Love is achieved when we learn to sacrifice ego.")
- **Action vs. Activity**: "Activity" is what a character does. "Action" is what they do TO GET SOMETHING from another person. Scenes should be driven by ACTION, not activity.
- **The Wound & The Want**: The character's WANT (external goal) is often a mask for their deeper NEED (healing the wound).

TRANSCRIPT EXCERPT:
The soul of your screenplay is not the story. The soul is what the protagonist comes to understand about themselves.
Every character has a spine—an unconscious belief that controls their behavior.
Activity is washing dishes. Action is washing dishes while waiting for a phone call that determines your fate.
The wound drives the want. The need heals the wound.

TITLE: Screenwriting: The Sequence Approach
AUTHOR: Paul Joseph Gulino
SOURCE: PDF Masterclass (8-Sequence Structure)
KEY_INSIGHTS:
- **The 8-Sequence Structure**: Feature films are not just 3 acts, but 8 sequences (each ~10-15 pages, with its own beginning/middle/end).
- **Sequence Breakdown**:
    1. *Sequence A (Setup)*: Introduce protagonist, world, and the "status quo."
    2. *Sequence B (Catalyst/Question)*: Inciting incident raises the dramatic question.
    3. *Sequence C (First Obstacle)*: Initial attempts to solve the problem.
    4. *Sequence D (Midpoint Complication)*: New information or event that changes the approach.
    5. *Sequence E (Raising Stakes)*: Deeper commitment, obstacles intensify.
    6. *Sequence F (Main Culmination / "All Is Lost")*: The hero's plan completely fails.
    7. *Sequence G (New Drive / Third Act Turn)*: A new understanding or approach emerges.
    8. *Sequence H (Resolution)*: The final confrontation and resolution.
- **Each Sequence Has a Mini-Goal**: Like a short film within the feature, with its own tension/resolution.
- **The Dramatic Question**: Every sequence should relate back to the central dramatic question established in Act 1.
- **Silent Film Origins**: This approach comes from early cinema, where each reel (~10 min) was a "sequence."

TRANSCRIPT EXCERPT:
Think of each sequence as a short film—it has its own tension and resolution.
The 8-sequence structure provides more granular checkpoints than the 3-act model.
The dramatic question, posed at the end of Sequence B, drives the entire narrative.
