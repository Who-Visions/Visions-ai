### **Workflow: The "Personal Brand Manager" Protocol**

**Goal:** To intervene when a clientâ€™s physical appearance (teeth, grooming, makeup) compromises the quality of the headshot, ensuring they leave with a usable product and improved professional confidence.

**Tools Required:**
*   **Tactful Communication:** The ability to have uncomfortable conversations.
*   **Grooming Kit:** Tweezers, scissors (for nose hairs/stray hairs).
*   **Professional Makeup Artist (MUA):** On-call or available for rescheduling.
*   **Rescheduling Policy:** Flexibility to move a shoot if the client is not camera-ready.

---

#### **1. The Mindset Shift (00:00 - 00:45)**
*   **Step 1:** Adopt the role of a **Personal Brand Manager**, not just a photographer.
*   **Step 2:** Acknowledge that your purpose is to help the client succeed in their industry, which sometimes requires pointing out flaws that others ignore.
*   **Step 3:** Aim for the "Ricochet Effect": Shoot for their head, but aim for their heart. Make them leave feeling cared for and confident.

#### **2. Scenario A: Dental Issues & Poor Grooming (00:52 - 04:17)**
*   **Identification:** Watch for clients holding back smiles to hide teeth, or visible distractions like nose hairs.
*   **The Intervention:**
    *   **Stop the shoot** or pause when you notice the issue.
    *   **The Script:** Tell the client, *"I can retouch this in the photo, but you have to walk into an audition/meeting in real life. You need to get this fixed for your personal brand."*
*   **Action Items:**
    *   **Nose Hairs:** Instruct the client to go to the restroom immediately with tweezers or scissors to clip them. (03:18)
    *   **Yellow/Crooked Teeth:** Advise them to get whitening or dental work.
*   **The "Over-Deliver" Strategy:** If the client agrees to get dental work done, offer to have them come back for a quick "one-look" session later to update the shot with their new smile. (04:00)

#### **3. Scenario B: Bad DIY Makeup & Hair (04:18 - 07:58)**
*   **Identification:** Look for "fugly" eyebrows, weird hairlines, uneven skin texture, or makeup that looks heavy/amateurish.
*   **The Test:** Shoot for 15â€“30 minutes. If the images are unusable due to styling, stop.
*   **The Intervention:**
    *   **The Script:** *"You are spending money to shoot with me. Do not waste this investment on bad makeup/hair."*
*   **Action Items:**
    *   **Stop the session.** Do not continue if the raw material is bad.
    *   **Mandate a Pro:** Insist they hire a professional Makeup Artist (MUA).
    *   **Reschedule:** Send them home and book a new time when the MUA is available.
*   **Result:** The client will likely feel relieved that you care enough about the quality to stop rather than taking their money for bad photos.

#### **4. Scenario C: The Bad Spray Tan (08:21 - 10:45)**
*   **Identification:** Look for orange/yellow skin tones, streak marks, or "burn marks" in the creases of the skin.
*   **The Intervention:**
    *   Refuse to shoot a client with a fresh, fake spray tan. It ruins skin tones and is difficult to fix in post-production.
*   **Action Items:**
    *   **Send them home.** Tell them to let the tan wear off.
    *   **Reschedule:** Bring them back when their skin tone is natural.
*   **Peterâ€™s Tanning Rule:** If a client *must* be tan, advise them to use a tanning bed lightly a week prior (to avoid redness) rather than spray tanning immediately before the shoot. *(Note: This is Peter's specific aesthetic advice for headshots).*

#### **5. Summary Rule: Under-Promise & Over-Deliver (08:02)**
*   Always prioritize the long-term result over the immediate session time.
*   If you have to cancel/reschedule to fix an appearance issue, frame it as ensuring they get the absolute best product.
*   **Key Takeaway:** If you don't mention the problem (teeth, nose hair, orange skin), the client will see it in the final photos and blame the photographer or feel bad about themselves. You must be the one to "call out the crap."
